Nvidia CEO **Jensen Huang** declared this week that artificial general intelligence (AGI) has been achieved. The claim would sound more impressive if anyone could agree on what AGI actually means.

## What Huang Said

At a recent event, Huang stated that AI has reached AGI-level capabilities. But as critics quickly pointed out, the statement hinges entirely on how you define AGI — and there's no consensus definition in the industry.

## The Definition Problem

AGI has been redefined so many times it's become almost meaningless:

- **Original definition**: AI that can perform any intellectual task a human can
- **Narrow interpretation**: AI that passes specific benchmarks
- **Marketing definition**: Whatever the latest model can do

As one commenter put it: *"Anything can be AGI if you're vague enough in defining it."*

## Why Huang's Words Matter

Regardless of the definitional debate, Huang's statement carries weight because:

1. **Nvidia dominates AI hardware** — their chips power virtually every major AI model
2. **Market implications** — AGI claims affect stock prices, investment, and policy
3. **Industry signaling** — Huang's confidence reflects Nvidia's view of the compute trajectory

## The Honest Answer

We're in a strange moment where AI can:
- Write code, essays, and research papers
- Pass bar exams, medical boards, and coding interviews
- Generate photorealistic images and videos
- Reason through complex multi-step problems

But it also:
- Hallucinates confidently
- Struggles with true common sense
- Can't learn from experience without retraining
- Lacks genuine understanding or consciousness

Whether that's "AGI" depends entirely on where you draw the line.

## What's Next

The real question isn't whether AGI has been achieved — it's whether the term still has any useful meaning. The industry may need to move beyond AGI as a concept and focus on specific, measurable capabilities instead.

The debate continues. The chips keep getting faster.
